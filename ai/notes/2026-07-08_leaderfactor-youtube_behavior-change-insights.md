# Look through all of the videos in this channel, and let me know what they suggest about how to practice a new habit, report on it, follow up, etc., to get the most success in making changes in behavior?

[https://www.youtube.com/@LeaderFactor/videos](https://www.youtube.com/@LeaderFactor/videos)

LeaderFactor’s content consistently suggests that the most reliable way to build new habits and change behavior is to treat behavior change as a measurable skill practiced in real work over ~90 days, inside a culture of psychological safety and clear accountability, rather than as one‑off “content” or motivation.[^1_1][^1_2]

Below is a distilled playbook drawn from their frameworks and episode descriptions, adapted for personal habit-building.[^1_3][^1_2][^1_1]

***

## Core philosophy: behavior, not content

LeaderFactor is explicit that “content alone doesn’t change behavior”; behavior changes when you build a skill, practice it in daily reps, and then measure the shift. Their programs combine a clear model (e.g., Coaching \& Accountability Matrix, 4 Stages of Psychological Safety) with structured practice and follow‑up assessment.[^1_2][^1_1]

They design everything around proving change at about 90 days (e.g., CAMindex™, PSindex® baseline and re‑test), which implies any habit you care about should be defined, practiced, and measured over that kind of time window, not just a week or two.[^1_1][^1_2]

***

## Precondition: psychological safety as “rewarded vulnerability”

Psychological safety is defined as a “culture of rewarded vulnerability”—people are rewarded, not punished, for the vulnerable acts of learning, contributing, and challenging the status quo. LeaderFactor repeatedly emphasizes that this culture is a prerequisite for real coaching and behavior change; without it, people hide mistakes and avoid experimentation.[^1_4][^1_5][^1_2][^1_1]

They describe four sequential stages—Inclusion Safety, Learner Safety, Contributor Safety, Challenger Safety—and argue that progress through these stages is what allows people to ask questions, try new behaviors, and challenge the status quo as part of habit change.[^1_6][^1_2]

Applied to your own habits: you need to create “learner safety” for yourself (and for any stakeholders) so experiments, imperfect execution, and honest reporting are welcomed rather than punished.[^1_7][^1_2]

***

## Designing the habit: be specific, outcome‑linked, and owned

LeaderFactor’s accountability content stresses that leaders “claim what they touch, operate in reality, and respond to mistakes with responsibility and eagerness to act.” In their “Accountability 100: Claim the result” material, they break this into behaviors like stepping forward early, declaring ownership clearly, and letting partners know you own the outcome.[^1_8][^1_9]

For a new habit, this implies:

- Define a single, outcome‑linked behavior (e.g., “replace one directive with a coaching question each day”), not a vague aspiration.[^1_1]
- Explicitly claim it: state to yourself or others, “I own the outcome of this habit,” and clarify what success looks like in observable terms.[^1_9][^1_8]
- Anchor it in reality by describing the current state honestly (where you’re falling short) before designing the change.[^1_8]

***

## Practice pattern: daily reps in real work for ~90 days

Their Coaching \& Accountability Matrix and micro‑coaching content are built on the idea of “real behavior change where learning becomes behavior.” The pattern they use:[^1_3][^1_1]

- Short pre‑work to understand the lever (e.g., why coaching matters, the business case).[^1_1]
- A small set of tools (e.g., Tell‑to‑Ask, question funnel, B.I.G. feedback, task‑to‑outcome accountability).[^1_10][^1_1]
- Then 90 days of “in‑workflow practice and coaching—reps inside the real work,” such as “replace one directive with a coaching question each day.”[^1_1]

Translating this to habit formation:

- Choose one micro‑behavior that can show up daily in your normal context (code reviews, meetings, planning, etc.).[^1_3][^1_1]
- Practice it once or a few times per day as part of what you already do, rather than in a separate “training” slot.[^1_1]
- Maintain this for a full 90‑day cycle to give the habit time to become default behavior.[^1_2][^1_1]

***

## Reporting and follow‑up: assess, learn, apply, prove

LeaderFactor’s skill design follows a four‑step loop: Assess → Learn → Apply → Prove. They:[^1_1]

- **Assess** with a baseline index (CAMindex™, PSindex®) that captures observable behaviors via self and external ratings.[^1_2][^1_1]
- **Learn** through focused cohort‑based modules targeted at gaps revealed by the assessment.[^1_1]
- **Apply** the frameworks to real direct reports or real workflows over 90 days, with daily reps.[^1_3][^1_1]
- **Prove** by re‑measuring on the same index to show movement “one box forward.”[^1_2][^1_1]

For your own habit, you can mimic this pattern:


| Phase | What you’d do personally |
| :-- | :-- |
| Assess | Create a 1–5 self‑rating and (optionally) have a peer rate you on the target behavior.[^1_1][^1_2] |
| Learn | Spend a few focused sessions clarifying the model or skill underpinning the habit.[^1_1] |
| Apply | Embed daily reps in normal work for ~90 days; keep a simple log (one line per day).[^1_1][^1_3] |
| Prove | Re‑rate yourself (and ask the same peer) on day 90 and compare to baseline.[^1_1][^1_2] |

The key idea: “behavior is measured, not assumed”—you don’t declare success because you consumed content; you show it via observable change over time.[^1_11][^1_1]

***

## How to talk about and report on the habit

LeaderFactor’s leadership episodes emphasize making expectations and accountability explicit, not fuzzy. They coach leaders to move from blame/denial/excuses to ownership by naming patterns of deflection and then coaching through them.[^1_12][^1_13][^1_10]

For reporting on your habit in a LeaderFactor‑style way:

- Use observable statements: “In the last 30 days, I asked coaching questions in 80% of my one‑on‑ones” rather than “I’m trying to coach more.”[^1_3][^1_1]
- Report both frequency (how often) and context (where), since their tools are “operationalized on a dynamic team” and focused on behavior in specific situations.[^1_3]
- When you fall short, avoid self‑coddling or self‑blame; instead, describe what happened in reality and what you’ll adjust—a form of accountable reflection they tie to healthy cultures.[^1_4][^1_8]

***

## Practical template you can use

Putting it all together, here’s a concrete pattern aligned with LeaderFactor’s approach that you can apply to any new habit (professional or personal):

1. **Name the habit and outcome.**
    - “I will do X behavior, in Y situations, so that Z outcome improves.”[^1_9][^1_8]
2. **Set a 90‑day window and baseline.**
    - Today: rate yourself 1–5, ask one trusted peer to rate you, and capture a brief description of current behavior.[^1_2][^1_1]
3. **Design daily reps in real work.**
    - Define a minimum daily rep (e.g., “one coaching question,” “one piece of B.I.G. feedback”) embedded in your normal workflow.[^1_3][^1_1]
4. **Create a lightweight reporting ritual.**
    - One line per day: date, did/didn’t, brief context and what you learned; review weekly for patterns.[^1_10][^1_1]
5. **Maintain psychological safety around the experiment.**
    - Treat mistakes as data, not moral failure; reward yourself for vulnerability (e.g., admitting a miss, trying a new variant).[^1_4][^1_2]
6. **Re‑assess and “prove” at 30 and 90 days.**
    - Re‑rate, re‑ask your peer, and compare; if needed, adjust the habit and start another cycle, just as they re‑run CAMindex™ or PSindex® to show movement.[^1_2][^1_1]
